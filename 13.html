TEACHER


A Teacher, also called a schoolteacher or formally an educator, is a person who helps students to acquire knowledge, competence, or virtue, via the practice of teaching.

1. Research-Oriented Professors

Research-oriented professors prioritize conducting original research in their field of expertise. They often have extensive publication records and may be highly focused on their research pursuits. They may teach courses related to their research interests and provide opportunities for students to participate in research projects.

2. Teaching-Focused Professors

Teaching-focused professors prioritize their role as educators and are dedicated to delivering high-quality instruction to their students. They may have a strong passion for teaching and employ various pedagogical techniques to engage students and facilitate learning. Teaching-focused professors often prioritize classroom interactions, student feedback, and mentoring.

3. Tenured Professors

Tenured professors are those who have earned tenure, a permanent appointment at the university. They have demonstrated exceptional expertise, research contributions, and teaching abilities over a significant period. Tenured professors often have more flexibility in their teaching and research pursuits and may have increased academic freedom.

4. Adjunct Professors

Adjunct professors are typically part-time faculty members who are hired on a contract basis. They often have specialized expertise in a particular field and may teach courses that align with their professional experience. Adjunct professors may have other professional commitments alongside their teaching responsibilities.

5. Clinical Professors

Clinical professors are often found in fields such as medicine, law, or other professional programs. They typically have practical experience and may continue to work in their profession alongside their teaching duties. Clinical professors often provide instruction in specialized skills, offer professional guidance, and supervise studentsâ€™ practical training.

6. Visiting Professors

Visiting professors are scholars or experts from other institutions who join a university for a limited period. They may bring unique perspectives, research collaborations, or specialized knowledge to the host institution. Visiting professors contribute to teaching, research, or both during their temporary appointment.

7. Emeritus Professors

Emeritus professors are retired professors who have been granted an honorary title in recognition of their past contributions to the institution. While they may no longer have full teaching or research responsibilities, emeritus professors may still engage in scholarly activities, mentor students, or provide guidance to the department.

Professors are responsible for providing high-level education and guidance in specific streams to students in colleges, universities and other institutions of higher learning. Being a professor can be a rewarding career, but it can take several years to fulfil the eligibility requirements. You may benefit from selecting educational goals that align with this career choice. How to become a professor

You might find the following steps helpful for becoming a college professor:

1. Decide your specialisation and earn a bachelor's degree

Decide early on if you want to become a professor in the arts, commerce or science stream and pass your 12th Higher Secondary Certification exam with at least 80-90% aggregate marks. This may help you enrol in a bachelor's degree programme of your choice. It is essential to maintain a high academic record and complete your graduation well above the minimum requirement of 50-55% aggregate marks.

Associate Professor Vs. Professor: What's The Difference?

2. Pursue a master's degree

Take the GATE exam, enrol in a master's degree programme of your choice and complete it with more than the minimum requirement of 50-55% aggregate marks. By clearing the master's degree programme with a good score, you may be eligible to work as a lecturer or a demonstrator. The work experience is likely to help you advance in your career.

3. Pass competitive exams

After getting a master's degree, you can appear for and pass the NET, the SET or the CSIR NET competitive exams. The NET makes you eligible for assistant professor jobs in every college and university throughout the country, but the SET only gives you eligibility for assistant professor jobs within the state you cleared the exam. By taking the CSIR NET, you may be selected for a lecturer position or get a junior research fellowship.

4. Obtain a doctoral degree

Next, you can enrol in a doctoral degree programme. Having a doctoral degree can exempt you from appearing for the competitive exams, and you can directly apply for a college professor position.
